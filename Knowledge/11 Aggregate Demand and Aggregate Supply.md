> Economist use the model of aggregate demand and aggregate supply to explain short-run fluctuations (i.e. [[9 Business Cycles]]) in economic activity around its long-run trend
> 
> - The *aggregate-demand* curve shows the quantity of goods and services that households, firms, and the government want to buy at each price level
> - The *aggregate-supply* curve shows the quantity of goods and services that firms choose to produce and sell at each price level

# AD
- The four components of GDP($Y$) contribute to the aggregate demand for goods and services
- AD不管short-run还是long-run，都是“价格水平 vs 总需求量”的同一关系。AD 永远来自这一个恒等式：
$$Y = C + I + G +NX$$
![[Screenshot 2026-01-05 at 16.28.03.png|400]]
## Why the Aggregate-Demand Curve Is Downward Sloping
- The Price Level and Consumption ($C$) - The Wealth Effect
    - An increase in the price level makes consumers feel less wealthy, which in turn encourages them to spend less
    - This decrease in consumer spending means smaller quantities of goods and services demanded
- The Price Level and Investment ($I$) - The Interest Rate Effect (**For euro area countries, the most important source of the downward slope of the AD**)
    1. Buying goods and services requires more money
    2. At given interest rate the demand for money increases
    3. Given the amount of money in circulation, the price of holding money needs to increase, i.e. the interest rate goes up, to new equilibrium interest rate
    4. makes borrowing more expensive, which discourages spending on investment goods
    5. This decrease in investment spending means a smaller quantity of goods and services demanded
    
    ![[Screenshot 2026-01-05 at 17.02.17.png]]
- The Price Level and Net Exports ($NX$) - The Exchange-Rate Effect
    - A higher price level in Switzerland causes (Swiss interest rates to rise and) 
    - the real exchange rate to appreciate
    - which depresses Swiss net exports
    - The decrease in net export spending means a smaller quantity of goods and services demanded
## Why the Aggregate-Demand Curve Might Shift
- (autonomous) Consumption
- (autonomous) Investment 
- Government Purchases 
- (autonomous) Net Exports 
- Money Supply
### Crowding-out Effect
- Fiscal policy may not affect the economy as strongly as predicted by the multiplier
    - An increase in government purchases causes the interest rate to rise
    - A higher interest rate reduces investment spending
- This reduction in demand that results when a fiscal expansion raises the interest rate is called the *crowding-out effect*
    - The crowding-out effect tends to dampen the effects of fiscal policy on aggregate demand

## Natural rate of output

Natural rate of output 
= the production of goods and services that an economy achieves in the long run when unemployment is at its normal rate
= Potential output
= Full-employment output ($Y_f$)

- The natural rate of output is the level of output towards which the economy gravitates in the long run i.e. 长期里，经济 **一定会回到这个产出水平**
## Long-Run Aggregate-Supply (LRAS) Curve
In the long run, the aggregate-supply curve is **vertical** because the price level does not affect long run determinants of real GDP
- 长期里，一国能生产多少东西，只取决于“*real variable*”。In the long run, an economy’s production of goods and services depends on its supplies of labour, capital, and natural resources and on the available technology used to turn these factors of production into goods and services
- 而价格水平（P）只是*nominal variable*
- The long-run aggregate supply represents the [[6 The Monetary System#The Classical Dichotomy and Monetary Neutrality|classical dichotomy and money neutrality]] 
- The long-run aggregate-supply curve is vertical at the natural rate of output
### Why the LRAS Curve Might Shift
- Any change in the economy that alters **the natural rate of output** shifts the long-run aggregate-supply curve 
- The shifts may be categorized according to the various factors in the classical model that affect output 
- Shifts might arise from changes in:
    **Everything changing production function, shifting the curve (Recall [[3 Production and Growth]])**
    - Labour (e.g. net immigration) 
    - Capital 
    - Natural Resources
    - Technological Knowledge (i.e. inventions of new technology)

## Short-Run Aggregate-Supply (SRAS) Curve
### Why the SRAS Curve Slopes Upward
#### The most simplest reason
- In the short run, an increase in the overall level of prices in the economy tends to raise the quantity of goods and services supplied
- A decrease in the level of prices tends to reduce the quantity of goods and services supplied
- As a result, the short-run aggregate-supply curve is upward sloping

#### The Sticky-Wage Theory
> Nominal wages are slow to adjust to changing economic conditions, or are “sticky” in the short run

- 当 价格水平 P ↓
- 名义工资 $W$ 还没降
- 实际工资 $W/P$ ↑
- 用工成本 ↑
- 企业发现 雇人更贵, 生产不划算
- the quantity of goods and services supplied ↓
#### The Sticky-Price Theory
> Prices of some goods and services adjust sluggishly in response to changing economic conditions. 不可能所有企业同时、立刻改价，有菜单成本、合约、协调问题

- 发生 意外的价格水平下降 ↓
- 一些企业价格还没来得及降，relative price“偏高”
- sales ↓，stock ↑
- quantity they produce ↓
#### The Misperceptions Theory
- Changes in the overall price level temporarily，but supplier 但分不清：是*整体deflation/inflation*，还是*相对价格变化*
- 当 P 下降：企业误以为“我这个行业需求变差了”
- 只是整体价格水平在下降(deflation)
- 企业 错误地减产

> [!summary] Summary ^sras-formula
> 
> $$
> \substack{\text{Quantity of}\\\text{Output Supplied}}
> =
> \substack{\text{Natural Rate}\\\text{of Output}}
> + a \times
> \left(
> \substack{\text{Actual}\\\text{Price Level}}
> -
> \substack{\text{Expected}\\\text{Price Level}}
> \right)
> $$
> 
> $$Y = \bar{Y} + a \left( P - P^{e} \right)$$
>
